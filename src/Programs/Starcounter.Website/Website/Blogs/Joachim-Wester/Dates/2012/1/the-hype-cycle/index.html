The Hype Cycle

January 20, 2012 by: Joachim Wester

Software people are bound by trends and tradition. Although some of us wants to think of ourselves as forward thinking and open minded, we are no better than our peers. You can pick a point of time in history at random and still rest assured that most buzzwords du jour sounds corny ten years later. And if you were amongst its loud voiced supporters, you would probably turn red hearing yourself advocate the latest-and-greatest.

At the time the trendy technology sounded so right, so new and so fresh. But just a few years later, the very same concepts will have seize /*seize?*/ to be in the center of attention. Some will be part of our everyday life and some will have left their mark as basis for the next thing and some will simply die. If we were there, as visitors from the future, we would tell the advocates of WAP, Enterprise Java Beans and ActiveX components that they might as well tone it down.

The thing they all have in common is that they never became "the-final-say"; the thing to replace that which was so wrong before. It was progress, but today, the big thing is always something else.

So do we believe that this time it is different. I think not. Cloud, MapReduce, HTML5 and NoSQL will to some degree survive the test of time, but they will resonate no different for the industry in 2022 then does client-server, servlets and Corba today. They left a mark, but they were soon replace as the proclaimed saviors. It's beginning to happen to things like XML and the Web 2.0.
# The Good News
The surviving technology, the fundamentally good ideas fall into two categories. The ones that are so fundamentally strong that they survive mostly everything. Things like the Internet, object oriented programming and HTML went from ultra-trendy buzzwords to institutions of our everyday lives.
## The hype cycle
We like to think about the smart-phones and tablets as unrelated to the Palm Pilots and Pen computers. We think Ruby is cool, but it is virtually another Smalltalk (some thirty years after). The phenomena has a name, it is called the hype cycle. Darwinism makes sure that good ideas survive
## Nature is cruel.
No matter how much something is hyped, it will not survive the tests of time unless it has some fundamental value. A few years before the web was born, the big giants, such as IBM, touted the future of software development. The buzzword was CASE (Computer Aided System Engineering), the idea was to draw lines between flowchart symbols to "draw" your entire application as a blueprint. The code was supposed to be generated from the drawings. CAD/CAM (Computer Aided Design/Computer Aided Manufacturing) had allowed engineers to make drawings of physical objects that would then be crafted by machines. The later was a survivor, but CASE soon died. The reality was that there was no reduction in complexity when you replaced text with pictures. You ended up with the same structures expressed in a different way. It turned out that writing and reading text was more efficient after all compared to using mouse and tablets to draw your software.
## The hype cycle is even crueler
Really, really good ideas are always reborn. Smalltalk was a fantastic idea, only to early. Computers were slow in the 80'es and it could never compete with C, the dominant language of the time. When the time was right, some decades later, object oriented languages with built in garbage collection such as Java, Javascript was born (Ruby is almost identical to its predecessor although it trendy life began around 30 years later). Products such as the Palm Pilot and the Pen Computers failed, but who remembers them when the iPhone and iPad changed our life some decades later.
## Looking into the future
I work with databases. Our industry is changing, and there is a lot to be learnt from the stories above. The relational database
replaced the document oriented databases /*I would say that they replaced hieararchical databases and network databases*/ that preceded them. They were left for dead. The object oriented databases were born
a decade or so after the birth of the object oriented languages. They failed as hard as the object oriented languages. Now every database, NoSQL or SQL has bindings to classes and instances. The first generation was born as ORM mappers (Object Relational Mapping).
Microsoft Entity Framework is the latest and greatest from Microsoft when it comes to database access. The newer NoSQL and graph databases allow for back-ends that are more friendly to the object oriented language front-end. The next generation databases are based on graphs and therefore does not see either classes, objects, tables, rows or documents as alien. But is this really new? I don't think so.
I would you to dedicate a thought to the document oriented databases of the 70'ies and the object oriented failures of the 80'ies the next time you start Cassandra, Entity Framework, Neo4J or Starcounter (my own creation). And no SQL and objects are no more dead than the Palm Pilot and the Pen Tablet, they are just being reborn as the iPhone and the iPad. And
just as the Pen Tablet and the iPhone was merged into a telephone, the new database will not have clunky OR mapping or be based on mechanical disk drives.
